Training a neural network means choosing a learning rate. Too small and training is too slow; too large and it diverges.

But something strange happens between these two extremes. For practical learning rates, the curvature of the loss landscape - the sharpness - slowly increases and inevitably crosses a critical threshold. At this threshold, the loss curve starts spiking erratically. Superficially, it seems like training will diverge.

And yet, it isn't. Zoom out, and the loss is still going down. The network is still learning. It has found a way to balance itself, training at what is called the edge of stability.

While neural network training is generally very difficult to analyze, it turns out we can understand several properties of this regime. In this article, we'll build up an explanation from first principles, run experiments directly in the browser, and see what this regime tells us about why certain training choices like adaptive optimizers work as well as they do.

Understanding Gradient Descent

We begin with gradient descent, and discuss modifications like SGD and adaptive gradients later. In Gradient descent, parameters $w_i$ are updated according to

$$w_{i+1} = w_i - \eta \nabla L(w_i)$$

where $\eta$ is the learning rate and $L$ is the loss. This is a simple rule, but analyzing its behavior on neural networks is not. To see this, consider the limit where $\eta \to 0$, where it becomes a continuous differential equation called gradient flow:

$$\frac{d w(t)}{dt} = - \nabla L(w(t))$$

A network with 100 parameters yields a 100-dimensional nonlinear differential equation where the different weight dynamics are nontrivially coupled. Analyzing this is hard even for small models with synthetic data. And this is ignoring the difficulties of nonzero learning rates, which introduce their own discretization instabilities on top of all of this.

Despite this, classical optimization theory gives us one useful foothold: if the learning rate is small enough relative to the curvature of the loss, gradient descent is guaranteed to decrease the loss at every step.[N] In other words, curvature determines how aggressively we can optimize.

Why does curvature matter? To build intuition, consider a 1D quadratic $L(w) = \frac{1}{2}Sw^2$, where $S$ controls the curvature. The update rule becomes

$$w_{i+1} = w_i - \eta S w_i \implies w_{n} = (1 - \eta S)^n w_0$$

which converges to the minimum $w = 0$ only if

$$|1 - \eta S| < 1 \implies S < \frac{2}{\eta}$$

If the learning rate is small relative to the curvature, gradient descent converges. If it's too large, the updates overshoot and oscillate around the minimum. If the curvature exceeds $2/\eta$, these oscillations grow, resulting in divergence.

For a multidimensional quadratic loss $L(w) = \frac{1}{2}w^\top A w$, where $A = \nabla^2 L$ is the Hessian, the update is

$$w_{i+1} = (I - \eta A)w_i$$

We can decompose $w$ along the eigenvectors of $A$. Each components amplitude $w_i^k$ (along eigenvector $\vec{k}$ with eigenvalue $\lambda_k$) evolves independently, exactly like the 1D case:

$$w_{i+1}^{k} = (1 - \eta \lambda_{k})w_{i}^{k}$$

Every component needs to converge, so the tightest constraint comes from the largest eigenvalue:

$$\lambda_{\max}(A) < \frac{2}{\eta}$$

We call the largest eigenvalue $\lambda_{\max}(A)$ the sharpness. It controls whether gradient descent converges or diverges on a quadratic, and as we saw in the opening figure, it accurately predicts when gradient descent on neural networks starts to behave erratically. This gives us the defining condition of the edge of stability: it occurs when the sharpness exceeds $2/\eta$.

Progressive Sharpening

This quadratic analysis has led past work to suggest the learning rate should be chosen by calculating the Hessian eigenvalue $\lambda_{\max}$. Setting $\eta = 1/\lambda_{\max}$ results in the fastest convergence on a quadratic, avoiding any oscillations (even those that slowly decay).

However, real loss landscapes are not quadratics. Their curvature varies over training, making this proposal impractical since it would require frequent and expensive Hessian computations to adapt to the changing curvature.

In fact, inspecting curvature over real training runs reveals an interesting trend - for many tasks, it tends to increase over time. We call this phenomenon progressive sharpening. It occurs across a large variety of tasks and models, and appears to be more pronounced for deeper networks.

While the reasons for progressive sharpening are not fully understood, the consequence is clear: most practical learning rates, which start out well behaved, will eventually reach the $2/\eta$ threshold. Learning rates that avoid this threshold are so small that training becomes impractically slow. This makes the edge of stability an inevitable part of training for any practical learning rate.

As you might have seen in the previous figure, something interesting happens at the $2/\eta$ threshold. The sharpness doesn't keep growing, but instead oscillates around the threshold. This phenomenon where the sharpness decreases after crossing the $2/\eta$ threshold is known as self-stability. It can be explained by going beyond classical theory with a third order Taylor expansion of the loss. To understand this, we'll go through a heuristic derivation for a single eigenvector at the edge of stability.

Beyond the critical threshold, gradient descent oscillates along the top Hessian eigenvector, $u$. Let $\hat{w}$ denote the point where we'd be if not for these oscillations. Because we have oscillations, our network is instead at

$$w = \hat{w} + x u$$

where $x$ is the amplitude of the oscillation. We can Taylor expand the gradient of the loss around $\hat{w}$ to understand how oscillations have changed our dynamics.

Second Order Taylor Expansion

As a refresher on vector Taylor expansions, imagine flattening our parameters into a vector in $\mathbb{R}^p$, and choosing one of the parameters $w_j$. The Taylor expansion for a single component is

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial w_j}(\hat{w} + x u) = \frac{\partial L}{\partial w_j} + \left[ \frac{\partial^2 L (\hat{w}) }{\partial w_1 \partial w_j} , \; \frac{\partial^2 L (\hat{w})}{\partial w_2 \partial w_j} , \; \dots, \; \frac{\partial^2 L (\hat{w}) }{\partial w_p \partial w_j} \right] (x u) + O(x^2 \|u\|^2)$$

Stacking these terms for all parameters gives us

$$ \nabla L(\hat{w} + x u) = \nabla L + \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial^2 L (\hat{w})}{\partial w_1 \partial w_1} & \frac{\partial^2 L (\hat{w})}{\partial w_2 \partial w_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial^2 L (\hat{w})}{\partial w_p \partial w_1} \\ \frac{\partial^2 L (\hat{w})}{\partial w_1 \partial w_2} & \frac{\partial^2 L (\hat{w})}{\partial w_2 \partial w_2} & \cdots & \frac{\partial^2 L (\hat{w})}{\partial w_p \partial w_2} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \frac{\partial^2 L (\hat{w})}{\partial w_1 \partial w_p} & \frac{\partial^2 L (\hat{w})}{\partial w_2 \partial w_p} & \cdots & \frac{\partial^2 L (\hat{w})}{\partial w_p \partial w_p} \end{bmatrix} (x u) + O(x^2 \|u\|^2) $$

The matrix in the second term is the Hessian at $\hat{w}$, $H_{ij}(\hat{w}) = \frac{\partial^2 L (\hat{w})}{\partial w_i \partial w_j}$. Thus our second order Taylor expansion is

$$\nabla L(\hat{w} + x u) = \nabla L(\hat{w}) + xH(\hat{w}) u + O(x^2 \|u\|^2)$$

Since $u$ is an eigenvector, $H(\hat{w}) u = \lambda u$ where $\lambda$ is the corresponding eigenvalue. This term causes oscillations to grow in magnitude when $\lambda > 2/\eta$.

Third Order Taylor Expansion

The third order term is more complicated. For a single component, it results in a matrix, and stacking all the components results in a 4d tensor. However, this tensor has a surprisingly simple interpretation. To see this, we write out the single component Taylor expansion as a summation

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial w_i}(\hat{w} + xu) = \frac{\partial L(\hat{w})}{\partial w_i} + x\sum_{j=1}^p \frac{\partial^2 L(\hat{w})}{\partial w_i \partial w_j} \cdot u_j + \frac{1}{2}x^2\sum_{j,k=1}^p \frac{\partial^3 L(\hat{w}) }{\partial w_i \partial w_j \partial w_k}\cdot u_j u_k + O(x^3 \|u\|^3)$$

Note that the sharpness is given by $\lambda(w) = u^T H(w) u = \sum_{j,k} u_j \frac{\partial^2 L}{\partial w_j \partial w_k} u_k$, since $u$ is a unit eigenvector of $H$. The gradient of the sharpness with respect to $w_i$ is

$$\frac{\partial \lambda}{\partial w_i} = \sum_{j,k} u_j \frac{\partial^3 L}{\partial w_i \partial w_j \partial w_k} u_k$$

and thus the Taylor expansion is

$$\nabla L(\hat{w} + x u) = \nabla L + x \lambda (\hat{w}) u + \frac{1}{2}x^2 \nabla \lambda (\hat{w}) + O(x^3 \|u\|^3)$$

So the third order term pushes gradient descent into a direction that reduces the sharpness, with strength proportional to the square of oscillations. In the unstable regime where oscillations increase in amplitude, this eventually reaches a point where this term dominates and the gradient points in a direction that reduces the sharpness, counteracting the effect of progressive sharpening.

When the sharpness is below the threshold again, oscillations shrink. Then progressive sharpening can occur again, pushing the sharpness back up.

These effects can be seen in the following diagram, which displays data from training 2000 samples of 5 class CIFAR10 on a small network. By moving the epoch slider, you can observe how the dynamics change as different terms in the Taylor expansion activate. We see that sharpness suppression very quickly takes effect when oscillations become a noticeable fraction of the gradient.

Summary

This completes our understanding of a single oscillating eigenvector at the edge of stability. The full trajectory is as follows:

  • The network has an initial loss and sharpness. The gradient tends to align with the sharpness gradient $\nabla \lambda$, causing sharpness to increase over time (progressive sharpening).
  • For many learning rates, the sharpness reaches the $2/\eta$ threshold. The component of the gradient along the largest eigenvector $u$ starts to oscillate. This causes spikes in the loss.
  • As oscillations grow, the third order Taylor term becomes stronger, pushing the trajectory in a direction that reduces sharpness.
  • As sharpness reduces, the oscillations shrink and the third order term weakens, allowing progressive sharpening to dominate again. The trajectory heads back to the edge of stability, where it oscillates again.
  • Throughout this process, the network makes steady progress to flatter regions of parameter space and lowers the loss overall, albeit with noisy spikes.

Other Eigenvalues

In general, more than one eigenvector undergoes progressive sharpening and ends up at the EOS. Empirically, we find the same self-stabilization occurs, but the dynamics are much more chaotic.

However, our previous analysis breaks down here. Let us consider a perturbation with two eigenvectors at the edge of stability, $\delta = x_1 u_1 + x_2 u_2$. The Taylor expansion up to second order gives

$$\nabla L(\hat{w} + \delta) = \nabla L(\hat{w}) + H(\hat{w})\delta + O(\|\delta\|^2)$$

Since $u_1$ and $u_2$ are eigenvectors of $H(\hat{w})$, this simplifies to

$$\nabla L(\hat{w} + \delta) = \nabla L(\hat{w}) + x_1 \lambda_1(\hat{w}) u_1 + x_2 \lambda_2(\hat{w}) u_2 + O(\|\delta\|^2)$$

Each eigenvector oscillates independently, just as in the single-eigenvector case. The complications arise at third order.

Please check this argument for accuracy.

Expanding the third-order term yields three contributions:

$$\frac{1}{2}x_1^2\nabla\lambda_1(\hat{w}) + \frac{1}{2}x_2^2\nabla\lambda_2(\hat{w}) + x_1 x_2\,\nabla(u_1^T H(\hat{w}) u_2)$$

The first two are the familiar sharpness-reducing terms from the single-eigenvector case. The third is a cross term that couples the two eigenvectors. Although $u_1^T H(\hat{w}) u_2 = 0$ by orthogonality, its gradient is generically nonzero, since the Hessian changes with $w$. This term means the self-stabilization of each eigenvalue depends on the oscillation amplitudes of the others, and the dynamics no longer decompose into independent systems.

Extending the self-stability analysis to multiple eigenvalues is an open question because of this.

Relation to Gradient Flow

Earlier, we mentioned that in the limit of infinitesimal learning rates, gradient descent becomes a continuous differential equation called gradient flow:

$$\frac{d w(t)}{dt} = - \nabla L(w(t))$$

Gradient flow is much easier to analyze than discrete gradient descent — it removes the discretization effects that cause oscillations and instability. But how well does it approximate real training at finite learning rates?

It turns out the approximation is excellent, when the sharpness stays below the $2/\eta$ threshold. In this regime, networks trained with different learning rates follow nearly identical trajectories in both loss and sharpness when plotted against rescaled time $\eta \cdot \text{step}$. You can verify this in the experiment below: run multiple learning rates with "η·step" plotting and observe that the curves overlap until each one hits its own stability threshold.

Once sharpness reaches $2/\eta$, the curves diverge. Each learning rate hits the threshold at a different point in rescaled time, and the EOS dynamics — oscillations, loss spikes, self-stabilization — take over. The edge of stability marks where gradient flow breaks down as a model of training.

How can we analyze optimization trajectories beyond this threshold? While the discrete dynamics are complicated and noisy, it turns out the analysis is quite nice if we average over the rapid oscillations at the edge of stability. This is the idea behind central flow,[3] which builds on the third-order Taylor analysis from the previous section. Using $\mathbb{E}$ to denote a time average over the oscillations, we obtain the following expression for the time averaged gradient:

$$\mathbb{E}\left[\nabla L(\hat{w} + x u)\right] = \underset{\text{gradient at time-averaged }w \approx \hat{w}}{\mathbb{E}\left[\nabla L (\hat{w}) \right]} + \underset{\text{time averages to 0}}{\mathbb{E}\left[x \lambda (\hat{w}) u \right]} + \underset{\text{sharpness penalty}}{\mathbb{E}\left[\frac{1}{2}x^2 \nabla \lambda (\hat{w}) \right]} + O(x^3 \|u\|^3)$$

Thus the time averaged gradient is the gradient at the time averaged parameter $\hat{w}$, plus a sharpness penalty that pushes the network towards flatter regions of parameter space. The oscillatory term averages to zero, so the mean trajectory is determined by the interplay between the original gradient and the sharpness penalty. The penalty is proportional to the square of the oscillation amplitude.

This motivates the following ansatz for the time-averaged trajectory of gradient descent:

$$ \frac{d \hat{w}(t)}{dt} = - \eta \left[\nabla L(\hat{w}(t)) + \underset{\text{sharpness penalty}}{\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 (t) \nabla \lambda (\hat{w}(t))} \right] $$

where $\sigma^2 (t)$ is a quantity that models the square of the oscillation amplitude. This unknown quantity can be solved for under the condition that the sharpness stays constant at the $2/\eta$ threshold.

This results in a simplified extension of gradient flow that models the mean trajectory of gradient descent even in the edge of stability regime. Optimizers And Related Phenomena

So far, we've analyzed full-batch gradient descent — the simplest optimization algorithm. In practice, neural networks are trained with momentum, adaptive optimizers, and stochastic minibatches. How do these modifications interact with the edge of stability?

Momentum

Momentum involves maintaining a velocity term that accumulates past gradients. This accelerates convergence along consistent gradient directions, and can help escape flat regions with low gradients. But it also has interesting interactions with curvature and the edge of stability. Its effects can be analyzed by studying the quadratic case again. Here, we'll go through Polyak (heavy ball) momentum, which is closely related to the momentum used in practical optimizers like Adam:

$$v_{t+1} = \beta v_t - \eta \nabla L(x_t)$$

$$x_{t+1} = x_t + v_{t+1}$$

The momentum coefficient $\beta$ controls how much past gradients are accumulated. For $\beta = 0$, this reduces to plain gradient descent.

On a 1D quadratic $L(x_t) = \frac{1}{2}Sx^2$, we use $\nabla L(x_t) = Sx_t$ and $v_t = x_t - x_{t-1}$ to obtain a second-order recurrence in $x_t$ alone:

$$x_{t+1} = (1 + \beta - \eta S)x_t - \beta x_{t-1}$$

This is a linear second-order recurrence. Standard results on such recurrences [3] show that it diverges when:

$$S > \frac{2(1+\beta)}{\eta}$$

which is larger than the standard edge of stability $2/\eta$. In this case, momentum makes it possible to train in regions of higher curvature. This extends to the multidimensional case, with the stability threshold again applying to the largest eigenvalue. The self-stabilization analysis from the previous section also applies beyond this threshold, ensuring the sharpness is fixed at the new critical threshold $\frac{2(1+\beta)}{\eta}$.

We can repeat this analysis for other types of momentum, which yield different results (e.g., Nesterov momentum yields $\frac{1}{\eta} \left( \frac{2 + 2 \beta}{1 + 2 \beta}\right)$, which means it is more unstable than vanilla GD).

Adam And Other Adaptive Optimizers

Adaptive gradient methods like Adam adjust their learning rates per-parameter based on recent gradient history. Why Adam is so effective at optimizing large neural networks may be partially explained by its interaction with the edge of stability.

Our analysis so far has assumed a single learning rate shared across all parameters. With per-parameter learning rates, the stability analysis changes: instead of the eigenvalues of the Hessian $H$, the relevant quantities are the eigenvalues of $PH$, where $P$ is a diagonal matrix of per-parameter learning rates. A properly chosen set of learning rates $P$ can in theory reduce the large eigenvalues while at the same time ensuring fast convergence among the smaller eigenvalues.

This turns out to be what adaptive optimizers do: by maintaining a running estimate of each parameter's gradient variance, they detect which parameters oscillate heavily, and automatically reduce the associated learning rates. This suppresses oscillations in directions that cause instability [3]. This is more effective than waiting for the slow self-stabilization mechanism to steer the network out of the unstable region, and may explain why Adam requires less learning rate tuning than plain gradient descent.

Stochastic Gradient Descent

Stochastic minibatches introduce noise into the gradient estimation. Although issues with large learning rates and stability still occur, the typical signs of edge of stability - loss spikes and a clear sharpness cutoff - are harder to detect since loss is inherently erratic under SGD, and there is plenty of noise in the per batch sharpness values.

On the theoretical side, much of our analysis assumed full-batch gradients, where the loss landscape is the same over mutliple steps. In SGD, this assumption is no longer valid, and our analysis of effects such as self-stability does not necessarily hold since the directions involved may change constantly.

On the empirical side, it has been observed that for fairly large batch sizes much of the previously discussed edge of stability behaviour still arises. We invite interested readers to explore with the interactive experiments at the bottom of this article.

Catapults

Throughout this article, we've assumed the learning rate is small enough that training starts in the stable regime, with sharpness gradually rising to meet the $2/\eta$ threshold. But what happens when the initial sharpness already exceeds $2/\eta$, without any progressive sharpening? There are two regimes depending on how large the learning rate is:[4]

  • Catapult phase: The learning rate is not too large, and the sharpness initially drops. The model is able to learn successfully here.
  • Divergent phase: The learning rate is too large, and the model diverges without learning anything.

The boundary between these regimes depends on the specific loss landscape and initialization. You can explore this in the playground below by setting a large initial learning rate and observing whether the network recovers or diverges.

Footnotes

On the Guaranteed Loss Decreases

More precisely, if $\nabla L$ is $M$-Lipschitz continuous (i.e., the curvature is bounded everywhere by $M$), then choosing $\eta < \frac{2}{M}$ guarantees monotone descent [3]. This is a global condition — it requires a learning rate conservative enough to work everywhere in parameter space. The $\frac{2}{\lambda_{\max}}$ threshold from the quadratic analysis is a local condition: it depends on the curvature at the current iterate, which changes during training.

It is possible for the local condition to be satisfied at every point visited, yet violated globally (e.g., an extremely sharp minimum exists far from the training trajectory, with curvature $>>M$).

On Hessian Eigenvalue Computation

The Hessian $\nabla^2 L$ is a $p \times p$ matrix of second derivatives, where $p$ is the number of parameters. Computing it fully and solving for all eigenvalues would be prohibitively expensive. Fortunately, we only need the top few eigenvalues and eigenvectors, which is significantly easier.

The simplest approach is power iteration. Start with a random vector $v_0$ — it will almost certainly have nonzero components along every eigenvector of the Hessian. Repeatedly multiplying by the Hessian (and normalizing) amplifies each component by its eigenvalue. After many iterations, the largest eigenvalue's component dominates, and $v_{k+1} = H v_k / \|H v_k\|$ converges to the top eigenvector. Finding the eigenvectors of a large $p \times p$ matrix is now replaced by a handful of matrix multiplications.

In practice, we use a more efficient algorithm called Lanczos iteration. Rather than discarding intermediate vectors, Lanczos retains the full history of vectors produced during each Hessian-vector product. This history can be used to more accurately obtain multiple top eigenvalues and eigenvectors. We run 20 Lanczos iterations, which can in principle recover up to 20 eigenvalues — though accuracy degrades for the smaller ones.

Note we never need the full Hessian matrix — only Hessian-vector products $Hv$. Since the Hessian is the Jacobian of the gradient, the product $Hv$ can be rewritten as a directional derivative of the gradient:

$$Hv = \lim_{\varepsilon \to 0} \frac{\nabla L(w + \varepsilon v) - \nabla L(w)}{\varepsilon}$$

This requires only two gradient evaluations rather than computing all $p^2$ entries of $H$. Our implementation approximates this with finite differences at $\varepsilon = 10^{-5}$:

$$Hv \approx \frac{\nabla L(w + \varepsilon v) - \nabla L(w - \varepsilon v)}{2\varepsilon}$$

This is computationally cheap and avoids second-order backpropagation. However, this produces issues with ReLU where the choice of $\varepsilon$ can cause spurious spikes due to the non-smoothness of the activation.
