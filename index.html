Gradient Descent — Quadratic Approximations

First, we'll discuss a traditional analysis of gradient descent. Under gradient descent, a model's initial parameters $w_0$ are updated according to

$$w_{i+1} = w_i - \eta \nabla L(w_i)$$

where $\eta$ is a step size (called the learning rate) and $L(w_i)$ the loss of our model with parameters $w_i$. In the limit $\eta \rightarrow 0$, this becomes a continuous differential equation called gradient flow, which is easier to analyze.

This leads to the following question: How do nonzero learning rates affect our optimization dynamics?

As an example, consider a 1D quadratic loss $L(w_i) = \frac{1}{2}Sw_i^2$. This yields the following update rule:

$$w_{i+1} = w_i - \eta S w_i \implies w_{n} = (1 - \eta S)^n w_0$$

which converges to the minima $w_i = 0$ only if

$$|1 - \eta S| < 1 \implies \eta < \frac{2}{S}$$

In higher dimensions, the quadratic loss is $L(w) = \frac{1}{2}w^\top A w$, where $A = \nabla^2 L$ is the Hessian, a $p \times p$ matrix where $p$ is the number of parameters. Since the gradient of this quadratic is $\nabla L(w) = Aw$, the update rule becomes

$$w_{i+1} = (I - \eta A)w_i$$

Since $A$ is symmetric, we can decompose the parameter vector along its eigenvectors. Writing $w_i^k$ for the component along the $k$-th eigenvector (with eigenvalue $\lambda_k$), each component evolves independently:

$$w_{i+1}^{k} = (1 - \eta \lambda_{k})w_{i}^{k}$$

This only converges when $|1 - \eta \lambda_k| < 1$, which must hold for every eigenvalue. The binding constraint comes from the largest:

$$\eta < \frac{2}{\lambda_{\max}(A)}$$

We call $\lambda_{\max}(A)$ the sharpness. If sharpness exceeds the critical threshold $2/\eta$, gradient descent diverges along the sharpest direction.

Local Stability

Real deep learning objectives are not quadratics, but we can always take a quadratic Taylor approximation around our current weights. If this approximation is reasonable, the stability condition $S(w) < 2/\eta$ should still apply locally — when the sharpness at the current weights exceeds $2/\eta$, we'd expect gradient descent to oscillate and diverge along the top Hessian eigenvector.

So why does gradient descent work in deep learning? One explanation from classical optimization theory is that gradient descent stays in "stable regions" of weight space - where $S(w) < 2/\eta$.

Edge of Stability and Progressive Sharpening

In real loss functions we tend to observe the following two phenomena:

  • Progressive Sharpening: When sharpness is less than $2/\eta$, it tends to continually increase throughout training.
  • Edge of Stability: When sharpness rises above $2/\eta$, gradient descent becomes unstable as predicted by the quadratic approximation.

Experiments: As an example, we train an MLP with a single hidden layer to a Chebyshev Polynomial (dataset depicted SOMEWHERE). Observe that the initial sharpness eventually increases to $2/\eta$, followed by instability.

The instability of training is manifests in the form of sudden spikes in the loss (in fact, one can prove that the loss has to go down for full-batch gradient descent if the loss function was smooth). However, at the edge of stability the sharpening behaviour appears to stop, with the sharpness instead oscillating around the critical threshold $2/\eta$. Furthermore, although the training loss is non-monotonic in the short term, there are signs of consistent progress over the long term.

This suggests networks self-stabilize at the critical sharpness threshold and are able to optimize at this boundary. We call these dynamics training at the edge of stability (EOS).

Open Question: Why do networks tend to sharpen during training? Although toy examples can be constructed without this behaviour, the trend appears often enough for realistic tasks to warrant investigation.

While we don't understand why networks tend to sharpen, we do understand the force that counteracts this tendency at the edge of stability. The third-order expansion of our training loss shows that oscillations along the top Hessian eigenvector automatically triggers a reduction of the top Hessian eigenvalue (colloquially, our model takes gradient dsteps to reduce the sharpness). The sharpness naturally decreases, forcing the network back into the region of stability.

These two effects also occur for other eigenvalues - they progressively sharpen, until they reach the threshold and self-stabilize. Multiple eigenvalues at this threshold results in extremely chaotic training curves, as you'll see in the next widget.

Given the edge of stability is dependent on the learning rate, should we choose sufficiently small learning rates (or dynamically chosen, based off hessian eigenvalues) to avoid the edge? The answer appears to be no, as this is sub-optimal for compute efficiency. Many realistic tasks require incredibly small learning rates to avoid the edge of stability, and are best optimized at the edge of stability instead. Thus an understanding of edge of stability dynamics appears essential to improving performance.

Relation to Gradient Flow

Earlier, we mentioned that gradient descent can be studied in the limit of infinitesimal learning rates as a continuous differential equation called gradient flow.

$$\frac{d w(t)}{dt} = - \nabla L(w(t))$$

This formulation simplifies the analysis of many systems. Returning to our original question, this simplification breaks down mostly at the edge of stability — up until hitting that threshold, different networks trained with different learning rates behave relatively similarly in their training loss and sharpness, and diverge only there. How can we describe the training dynamics of neural networks beyond this threshold? One idea, related to earlier work explaining self-stabilization via the third-order Taylor expansion is called central flow. The idea is that you can time-average out the oscillations in the training dynamics. This leads to an equation where progressive sharpening and self-stabilizing terms cancel to lead to a simple description of average dynamics. For the curious reader, we refer them to this blog post.

Observations On The Edge Of Stability And Progressive Sharpening

Although the existence of the edge of stability (from second order Taylor arguments) and the self stabilizing effect at the edge (from the third order expansion) are well established, there is a lot that is unknown about this phenomenon. Here, we give a rough empirical overview of how the phenomenon changes across different but common machine learning scenarios, and some open questions that arise from these observations.

To back up our claims when possible and encourage exploration, we provide settings to show this in the widget at the bottom, or provide graphs from existing literature when the experiment would be too computationally expensive.

Learning Rate

This is the most important parameter (due to predicting the edge of stability). We note that although choosing large learning rates is optimal for compute efficiency, there are also issues with choosen learning rates too large (SEE: catapult phenomenon).

Activation Function

Throughout this tutorial, we've focused on tanh networks even though ReLU networks are more common in practice. This choice is because ReLU networks seem to have more intense oscillations, with a critical threshold a little lower than the predicted $2/\eta$. A plausible explanation is the non-smoothness of ReLU, making the local dynamics predicted by a quadratic approximation less accurate. Aside from this, it has unclear effects on initial sharpness and the rate of progressive sharpening.
